What You Need to Know About These Courses
One-line engineering assessment of every course in this plan
Calculus 3
Extends Calculus 1 & 2 into multivariable territory — essential for fields, electromagnetics, and signals. Every serious ECE topic in Year 3 and 4 will silently assume this material.
Electric Circuits
Opens the most future courses in the ECE tree — prioritize this above all else. Electronics, Power Systems, Electromagnetics, and Signals all depend on the intuition you build here.
Digital Logic
Foundational for computer architecture — understand it deeply, do not memorise. Every gate, flip-flop, and state machine here will reappear in CAO, VLSI, and embedded systems.
Physics Lab 1 or 2
Low-risk add-on — manageable alongside your main load if you study consistently. The credit-to-effort ratio is favourable in summer.
Computer Skills for Engineers
Must be cleared early — it is a gateway course with downstream registration dependencies. Delaying it compounds into scheduling problems that affect later semesters.
Elective
Strategic breathing room — choose something you can genuinely excel in to protect your GPA. This is not slacking; it is load-balancing.
Courses First-Years Must Not Take
These are not hard — they require maturity you have not yet had time to build
⛔
Do Not Take as a First-Year
Computer Architecture & Organization 1 (CAO1)
You do not yet have the engineering mindset or problem-solving foundation this course demands. Taking it now risks either failing or extracting zero lasting knowledge — and this course is foundational to everything that follows.
Probability and Random Processes
This course requires mathematical maturity built over multiple semesters. Attempting it in your first year will result in surface-level understanding of a course that underpins communications, signal processing, and machine learning.
Even the student who authored this plan — known for taking the department's hardest semesters — did not attempt these as a first-year. That decision was deliberate, and it paid off.
Engineering is Not Science Faculty
The rules have changed — update your method before Week 1 begins
Your Study Approach Must Change.

The techniques that earned you high grades in science-track courses — reading the night before, cramming before exams — will cause you to fail or suffer significantly in engineering. The material is cumulative, problem-solving-heavy, and cannot be compressed into short sessions. Consistent, structured, weekly study is not optional — it is the only method that works.

01
Consistency Over Intensity
Study every day, not in bursts before exams. Engineering material is built in layers — each session reinforces the previous one.
02
Problem-First Learning
Do the problems before you feel ready. The confusion you hit in problems is exactly what clarifies the theory.
03
Build Foundations
Every course you skip understanding in will cost you two courses later. A grade earned without understanding is a liability.
