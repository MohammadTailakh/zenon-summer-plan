What You Need to Know About These Courses
One-line engineering assessment of every course in this plan
Calculus 3
Extends Calculus 1 & 2 into multivariable territory — essential for fields, electromagnetics, and signals. Every serious ECE topic you encounter in Year 3 and 4 will silently assume this material.
Electric Circuits
Opens the most future courses in the ECE tree — prioritize this above all else. Electronics, Power Systems, Electromagnetics, and Signals all depend on the intuition you build here. Do not take it passively.
Digital Logic
Foundational for computer architecture — understand it deeply, do not memorise. Every gate, flip-flop, and state machine you study here will reappear in a more complex form in CAO, VLSI, and embedded systems.
Physics Lab 1 or 2
Low-risk add-on — manageable alongside your main load if you study consistently. The credit-to-effort ratio is favourable in summer. Useful for building physical intuition before you see the theory formally.
Computer Skills for Engineers
Must be cleared early — it is a gateway course with downstream registration dependencies. Delaying it compounds into scheduling problems that affect later semesters, not just this one.
Elective
Strategic breathing room — choose something you can genuinely excel in to protect your GPA. This is not slacking; it is load-balancing while you are still building engineering study habits from scratch.
Courses First-Years Must Not Take
These are not hard — they require maturity you have not yet had time to build
⛔
Do Not Take as a First-Year
Computer Architecture & Organization 1 (CAO1)
You do not yet have the engineering mindset or problem-solving foundation this course demands. Taking it now risks either failing or extracting zero lasting knowledge — and this course is foundational to everything that follows in the computer engineering track. A weak pass here will cost you heavily in CAO2, Microprocessors, and Embedded Systems.
Probability and Random Processes
This course requires mathematical maturity built over multiple semesters. Attempting it in your first year will result in surface-level understanding of a course that underpins communications, signal processing, and machine learning — three of the highest-value domains in the ECE field. The loss of depth here echoes for years.
Even the student who authored this plan — known for taking the department's hardest semesters — did not attempt these as a first-year. That decision was deliberate, and it paid off. Maturity in engineering is not about how fast you move; it is about how well you understand each layer before building the next one on top of it.
Engineering is Not Science Faculty
The rules have changed — update your method before Week 1 begins
Your Study Approach Must Change.

The techniques that earned you high grades in science-track courses — reading the night before, cramming before exams — will cause you to fail or suffer significantly in engineering. The material is cumulative, problem-solving-heavy, and cannot be compressed into short sessions. Consistent, structured, weekly study is not optional — it is the only method that works. The engineer who studies two hours every day outperforms the one who studies fourteen hours the night before the exam, every semester, without exception.

01
Consistency Over Intensity
Study every day, not in bursts before exams. Engineering material is built in layers — each session reinforces the previous one. A single overnight session cannot replicate this compounding effect.
02
Problem-First Learning
Do the problems before you feel ready. Waiting until you fully understand the theory before attempting problems is an engineering student's most expensive mistake. The confusion you hit in problems is exactly what clarifies the theory.
03
Build Foundations
Every course you skip understanding in will cost you two courses later. A grade earned without understanding is a liability — it will appear as a gap precisely when the stakes are highest, in your third or fourth year.
